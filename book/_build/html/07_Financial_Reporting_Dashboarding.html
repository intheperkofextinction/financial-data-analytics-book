Chapter 7: Financial Reporting & Dashboarding#

Chapter 7.1 — KPI Selection#

What Are KPIs?#

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) are a small set of metrics that reflect the financial health and performance of a business.

In financial reporting, KPIs answer one question:

Are we moving in the right direction financially?

Good dashboards are built around KPIs — not raw data.

Why KPI Selection Matters#

Executives do not have time to analyze every number.

Poor KPI selection leads to:

  • Confusion

  • Misaligned decisions

  • Focus on the wrong problems

Strong KPI selection:

  • Aligns reporting with strategy

  • Highlights what truly matters

  • Enables faster decisions

Characteristics of Good Financial KPIs#

Effective financial KPIs are:

  • Relevant to business goals

  • Easy to understand

  • Actionable

  • Consistent over time

  • Limited in number

If a KPI cannot drive a decision, it does not belong on a dashboard.

Common Financial KPIs by Category#

Profitability

  • Gross Margin

  • Operating Margin

  • Net Profit Margin

Liquidity

  • Current Ratio

  • Cash Conversion Cycle

Efficiency

  • Revenue per Employee

  • Asset Turnover

FP&A / Performance

  • Budget vs Actual %

  • Forecast Accuracy

import pandas as pd

data = {
    "KPI": ["Revenue", "Operating Margin", "Operating Profit"],
    "Actual": [980000, 0.18, 176400],
    "Target": [1000000, 0.20, 200000]
}

df_kpi = pd.DataFrame(data)
df_kpi["Variance"] = df_kpi["Actual"] - df_kpi["Target"]

df_kpi
KPI Actual Target Variance
0 Revenue 980000.00 1000000.0 -20000.00
1 Operating Margin 0.18 0.2 -0.02
2 Operating Profit 176400.00 200000.0 -23600.00

Interpreting KPIs for Reporting#

Key reporting insights:

  • Revenue below target signals demand pressure

  • Margin decline amplifies profit impact

  • Profit variance combines revenue and cost effects

Dashboards should highlight these insights visually, not expect executives to calculate them mentally.

KPI Overload: A Common Reporting Trap#

More KPIs do not mean better reporting.

Too many metrics:

  • Dilute focus

  • Slow decision-making

  • Reduce clarity

Strong dashboards typically show 5–10 core KPIs.

Section Takeaway#

KPI selection is the foundation of effective financial dashboards.

Choose KPIs that:

  • Reflect strategy

  • Signal problems early

  • Support decisions

Everything else is supporting detail.

Chapter 7.2 — Financial Storytelling#

What Is Financial Storytelling?#

Financial storytelling is the ability to explain financial data in a way that leads to understanding and action.

It answers this question for leadership:

What is the story behind the numbers?

Numbers alone inform.
Stories drive decisions.

Why Financial Storytelling Matters#

Executives are not analysts.

Without context, dashboards become:

  • Confusing

  • Misleading

  • Easy to ignore

Effective storytelling:

  • Connects KPIs to business reality

  • Highlights cause-and-effect

  • Reduces decision fatigue

Structure of a Financial Story#

Most strong financial narratives follow this structure:

  1. What happened?

  2. Why did it happen?

  3. What does it mean?

  4. What should we do next?

This structure mirrors how executives naturally think.

From KPIs to Narrative#

KPIs are the inputs. Storytelling is the output.

Example:

  • KPI: Revenue down 2%

  • Story: Demand softened in one region while costs remained fixed, leading to margin pressure.

FP&A exists to bridge this gap.

Example: Turning Data into a Story#

Data Observation:

  • Revenue below plan

  • Operating margin declined

  • Fixed costs unchanged

Story: Revenue softness reduced operating leverage. Without cost flexibility, profit declined faster than revenue.

Implication: Short-term margin pressure will continue unless volume recovers or costs are adjusted.

import pandas as pd

data = {
    "Metric": ["Revenue", "Operating Margin", "Operating Profit"],
    "Actual": [980000, 0.18, 176400],
    "Budget": [1000000, 0.20, 200000]
}

df_story = pd.DataFrame(data)
df_story["Variance"] = df_story["Actual"] - df_story["Budget"]

df_story
Metric Actual Budget Variance
0 Revenue 980000.00 1000000.0 -20000.00
1 Operating Margin 0.18 0.2 -0.02
2 Operating Profit 176400.00 200000.0 -23600.00

Analyst Interpretation (Story Layer)#

Key narrative points:

  • Revenue decline is modest but impactful

  • Margin compression magnifies profit impact

  • Cost rigidity limits short-term recovery

This narrative guides management attention without overwhelming them with detail.

Common Financial Storytelling Mistakes#

  • Repeating numbers without interpretation

  • Overusing technical language

  • Avoiding clear conclusions

  • Failing to link data to decisions

If leadership asks “So what?”, the story is incomplete.

Storytelling Takeaway#

Strong financial storytelling:

  • Simplifies complexity

  • Highlights drivers

  • Encourages action

The best analysts are remembered for clarity, not calculations.

Chapter 7.3 — Power BI / Reporting Concepts#

Why Power BI Matters in Financial Reporting#

Power BI is not just a visualization tool. In finance teams, it is a decision-support platform.

FP&A and finance teams use Power BI to:

  • Monitor KPIs in real time

  • Track Budget vs Actual performance

  • Identify trends and anomalies

  • Communicate insights clearly to leadership

The value of Power BI lies in how data is modeled and presented, not just in charts.

Power BI vs Excel Reporting#

Excel Reporting

Power BI Reporting

Static

Interactive

Manual refresh

Automated refresh

Sheet-based

Model-based

Analyst-driven

Self-service for leadership

Power BI reduces repetitive reporting and allows analysts to focus on insights.

Core Components of Power BI#

A typical Power BI solution consists of:

  1. Data Sources

    • Databases, CSV files, APIs, Excel

  2. Data Model

    • Tables, relationships, measures

  3. Visual Layer

    • Charts, tables, KPIs, filters

Strong reporting starts with a strong data model.

Importance of the Data Model (Finance View)#

In financial reporting, poor data models lead to:

  • Incorrect KPIs

  • Slow dashboards

  • Confusing results

Good financial data models:

  • Separate facts (numbers) from dimensions (time, entity)

  • Use consistent definitions

  • Support multiple views (monthly, YTD, YoY)

Finance trusts dashboards only when numbers reconcile.

Common Financial Visuals in Power BI#

Widely used visuals in financial dashboards include:

  • KPI cards (Revenue, Margin, Profit)

  • Line charts (Trends over time)

  • Bar charts (Actual vs Budget)

  • Tables with conditional formatting

  • Waterfall charts (Variance breakdown)

Visual choice should support the message, not distract from it.

Dashboard Design Principles (FP&A Focus)#

Effective financial dashboards follow these principles:

  • Show KPIs at the top

  • Use trends instead of single-period numbers

  • Keep layouts simple and consistent

  • Avoid unnecessary colors and decorations

If a dashboard needs explanation, it is already too complex.

import pandas as pd

data = {
    "Month": ["Jan", "Feb", "Mar"],
    "Revenue": [320000, 330000, 330000],
    "Operating_Profit": [58000, 60000, 58400]
}

df_powerbi = pd.DataFrame(data)
df_powerbi
Month Revenue Operating_Profit
0 Jan 320000 58000
1 Feb 330000 60000
2 Mar 330000 58400

How This Data Appears in Power BI#

This dataset would typically be used for:

  • Line charts showing revenue and profit trends

  • KPI cards displaying YTD performance

  • Filters for time-period selection

Power BI handles the visualization. FP&A defines what should be visualized.

Common Power BI Reporting Mistakes#

  • Replicating Excel sheets inside Power BI

  • Overloading dashboards with visuals

  • Ignoring financial definitions

  • Focusing on visuals over insights

A beautiful dashboard with wrong numbers destroys trust.

Section Takeaway#

Power BI is a powerful reporting tool, but its effectiveness depends on financial thinking.

FP&A professionals succeed when they:

  • Design clean data models

  • Select meaningful visuals

  • Communicate insights clearly

Tools support analysis — they do not replace judgment.

Chapter 7.4 — Executive-Level Insights#

What Are Executive-Level Insights?#

Executive-level insights are conclusions drawn from financial data that directly support leadership decisions.

They are not detailed analysis. They are clear, focused, and actionable interpretations.

Executives care less about how numbers were calculated and more about what the numbers imply.

How Executives Consume Financial Information#

Executives typically:

  • Scan dashboards quickly

  • Focus on exceptions and trends

  • Ask high-level “why” questions

They expect:

  • Simplicity

  • Clarity

  • Confidence in the numbers

FP&A must anticipate these expectations.

Translating Analysis into Insights#

Analysis becomes an insight when it answers:

  • What changed?

  • Why did it change?

  • What risk or opportunity does this create?

  • What decision does this enable?

If these questions are unanswered, the work remains analytical, not strategic.

Example: From KPI to Executive Insight#

KPI Observation: Operating margin declined by 2%.

Analysis: Revenue growth slowed while fixed costs remained unchanged.

Executive Insight: Margin pressure is driven by volume softness, not cost inefficiency. Short-term cost flexibility is limited.

Decision Implication: Focus on demand recovery rather than aggressive cost cuts.

Framing Insights for Leadership#

Strong executive insights are:

  • Short (1–2 sentences)

  • Confident, not speculative

  • Supported by data

  • Focused on decisions, not explanations

Avoid overqualifying insights with excessive detail.

import pandas as pd

data = {
    "Metric": ["Revenue", "Operating Margin", "Operating Profit"],
    "Actual": [980000, 0.18, 176400],
    "Previous_Period": [1000000, 0.20, 200000]
}

df_exec = pd.DataFrame(data)
df_exec["Change"] = df_exec["Actual"] - df_exec["Previous_Period"]

df_exec
Metric Actual Previous_Period Change
0 Revenue 980000.00 1000000.0 -20000.00
1 Operating Margin 0.18 0.2 -0.02
2 Operating Profit 176400.00 200000.0 -23600.00

Executive-Level Interpretation#

Key insights:

  • Revenue softness is modest but persistent

  • Margin compression amplifies profit decline

  • Operating leverage increases downside risk

These insights guide strategic focus without requiring executives to inspect raw data.

What Executives Do NOT Want#

Executives generally do not want:

  • Step-by-step calculations

  • Technical explanations

  • Spreadsheet-style detail

  • Unclear conclusions

FP&A should bring clarity, not complexity.

Section Takeaway#

Executive-level insights sit at the intersection of:

  • Financial accuracy

  • Business understanding

  • Clear communication

Analysts who master this skill become trusted advisors, not just report builders.

Chapter 7.5 — Common Reporting Mistakes#

Why Reporting Mistakes Matter#

Financial reporting errors do not just confuse readers. They reduce trust.

Once leadership loses confidence in a report, even correct insights may be ignored.

Avoiding reporting mistakes is as important as producing accurate analysis.

Mistake 1: Too Much Data, Too Little Insight#

Common symptoms:

  • Large tables with no interpretation

  • Dashboards crowded with visuals

  • Excessive KPIs

Executives need conclusions, not raw data.

If everything is shown, nothing stands out.

Mistake 2: No Business Context#

Reporting numbers without context leads to misinterpretation.

Example:

  • Revenue down 3%

Without context, this could indicate:

  • Seasonality

  • Market conditions

  • Competitive pressure

FP&A must always provide context alongside metrics.

Mistake 3: Aesthetics Over Accuracy#

Visually appealing dashboards are valuable, but accuracy comes first.

Common issues:

  • Inconsistent definitions

  • Incorrect aggregations

  • Numbers that do not reconcile

A simple, accurate report is better than a beautiful but unreliable one.

Mistake 4: Ignoring the Audience#

Different stakeholders need different views.

Mistakes include:

  • Technical explanations for executives

  • Oversimplified views for analysts

  • One-size-fits-all dashboards

Effective reporting adapts to the audience.

Mistake 5: Reporting Without Recommendations#

A report that ends with numbers is incomplete.

FP&A reports should answer:

  • What happened?

  • Why did it happen?

  • What should we do next?

Without recommendations, reports become passive.

How to Avoid These Mistakes#

Strong financial reporting practices:

  • Start with key questions

  • Limit KPIs to what matters

  • Add interpretation and context

  • Validate numbers before publishing

  • End with clear recommendations

Section Takeaway#

Most reporting problems are not technical. They are conceptual.

Clear thinking, strong context, and decision focus prevent most mistakes.
